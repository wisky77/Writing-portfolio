The Weight of Waiting

It’s one in the morning. Sleep is an idea, not a reality.
Something lingers in the air.
Not smoke. Not sirens.
Something heavier — something that drifts through closed windows and settles deep in the bones.
It doesn’t shift with the wind. It doesn’t leave when the screens go dark.

You scroll.
And the timeline bleeds:
Youth with dreams turned headlines,
Children caught in crossfire,
Mothers clutching grief that language cannot hold,
A nation pacing between protests and prayers.

What is this thing that wakes us?
That hushes sleep and stirs silence into static?

The country is ailing — and the pain isn’t whispered anymore.
It’s screamed in alleys, echoed in hashtags, folded into coffins.
I mourn the lives we lost before they ever lived.
The dreams aborted by bullets, the futures that never learned to spell hope.

Mainstream media whispers about peace.
But behind closed doors, abduction is the dialect of power.
Bodies surface with wounds that speak louder than broadcasts.
And if you think that’s where the horror stops — brace yourself.

The jaw-dropper?
A shoot-to-kill order.
Not barked by rogue officers.
But issued by the very leaders meant to shield us.
Elected voices now echo like threats.
And somehow, we’re told this is stability.

So we wait.
Not because we trust the silence,
but because we’ve run out of ways to scream.

How long does a nation hold its breath
before it forgets how to exhale?

The streets are tired.
The names are many.
The questions echo louder than the answers.

And still — something stirs.
A shift. A whisper. A pulse beneath the pavement.

When will it end?
No one knows.
But if it does,
may it end with truth,
not just quiet.
